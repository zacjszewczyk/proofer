Artificial Intelligence at the Tactical Cyber Edge

Back in 2019, Rudy Guyonneau and Arnaud Le Dez captured a common fear in Artificial Intelligence in Digital Warfare: “The question of AI now tends to manifest under the guise of a mythicized omniscience and therefore, of a mythicized omnipotence. This can lead to paralysis of people fearful of having to fight against some super-enemy endowed with such an intelligence that it would leave us bereft of solutions.” In 2022, when ChatGPT exploded onto the scene, it looked like that fear had come true. Through accelerating the pace of analysis, improving warning intelligence, more efficient education, and more realistic training, artificial intelligence could have a drastic impact on defensive cyber operations today.

The State of Play of Artificial Intelligence in Cyber Operations #

Microsoft and OpenAI found five threat actors from four countries using artificial intelligence. At the start of their campaigns, the groups used large language models for target research, as translators, to build tools, and to write content for phishing campaigns. Later, Microsoft saw the models recommending post-compromise actions. Although some research has proposed that modern models could take on more, that seems premature. Rather than the letdown of an over-hyped promise, though, we ought to view these case studies as early examples of proper uses for artificial intelligence in offensive cyber operations. In stark contrast to ideas that artificial intelligence would unleash a wave of robot hackers on the world, these actors used the tools to automate mundane tasks. Defensive cyber forces, on the other hand, could use this technology in a number of impactful ways, starting with accelerating the pace of analysis.

Accelerating the Pace of Analysis #

Endpoints and network sensors create hundreds of billions of events per day across the Department of Defense’s Information Network. Indeed, “data overloadisnot a danger but rather a given.” Cyber analysts must not only sift through oceans of data, though, but they must also grapple with “techniques and strategies [that] evolve at a frantic pace, the former through the exigence imposed by early experiences in the field and the rate of technological development, the latter as our understanding of the stakes grows.” Itisnot just the volume of data in the fifth domain that confounds understanding, but it’s complexity as well. This ocean of uncertaintyisa prime target for two of the most common forms of artificial intelligence, machine learning and large language models.

While machine learning will never turn data into knowledge on its own, it can accelerateConsider replacing with: speed up the pace of analysis. These algorithms might not grasp why an endpoint behaves abnormally, but they excel at flagging unusual activity for human investigation. Applied at scale, such a pairing could shift the burden of looking through millions of logs onto a computer while freeing up humans for more nebulous investigative work. The complexity of training, tuning, evaluating, employing, and interpreting the output of these algorithms, though, means that few use them well, if at all. Large language models could lower that bar. ChatGPT or the open source Llama 3, for instance, can abstract away those tedious, error-prone steps. Instead of spending my time writing code for a random forest regression, for example, I can tell ChatGPT to “Build a random forest regression based on this sample data set.” Instead of spending my time digging through pages of complex documentationConsider replacing with: papers, documents to tune the algorithm’s hyperparameters, I can tell Llama 3 to optimize them. Tasks that might have taken the rare data scientist hours to days to achieve can now take an enterprising analyst just a few minutes.

Cyber analysts start many investigations based on opaque, machine-generated alarms. For example, an alert that “Trojan!Win32” malware could have infected an endpoint might entail hours of work just to gather basic information. Improved Warning Intelligence #

Machine learning could also help analysts derive meaning from internet-wide scanning data for improved warning intelligence. Traditional intelligence processes have struggled to keep pace with the cyber domain. Many reports on servers used to launch attacks, command and control malware implants, or receive exfiltrated data arrive far too late to do any good. They provideConsider replacing with: give interesting but seldom actionable information. By extracting those servers’ traits from internet-wide scanning data, though, and then training models to recognize them, cyber analysts could deploy models against live data feeds to proactively identify new malicious infrastructure in near-real time. Rather than acting on similar insights in days or weeks as reports make their way out of an intelligence cycle, this strategy presents a promising avenue to operationalize intelligence in the cyber domain at machine speed.

More Efficient Education #

While operational use cases for artificial intelligence abound, it could also enableConsider replacing with: allow better preparation for those defensive cyber missions, too. Creating, running, and managing training, for example,isextremely time consuming and exceptionallyConsider replacing with: only when, in this case difficult to do well. I dealt with this challenge just last year in the fledgling 3rd Multi-Domain Task Force. Stood up without any way to train its sizeable cyber formation, we found ourselves once again re-creating the wheel by building our own training program. We planned to spend over a year on this project. After some experimentation, though, we found a way to use large language models to create the entire curriculum — to include lessons plans, training materialConsider replacing with: relevant, practical exercises, and assessments — in just a few hours.

Another project used large language models to build threat hunting and investigation playbooks based on MITRE’s ATT&CK matrix, an industry standard taxonomy that describes malicious activities in the cyber domain. The threat hunting notebooks first describe actions a threat actor might take when attacking a network, then detail ways to find evidence of that activity. The investigation playbooks contain complementary guidance for investigating that evidence. Together, these documents promote analytic rigor by helping even the most junior analysts to approach these complex problems in a similar way as their more experienced counterparts. Over the years I have seen many groups pursue that lofty goal. Given that the MITRE ATT&CK matrix consists of 625 techniques, though, all have burnt out long before achieving it. The process of researching, understanding, and then developing detection mechanisms and investigation processes for such a vast array of malicious activities would have taken me months, if I had ever completed it at all. A large language model generated these playbooks, though, based on a template and a handful of examples, in a few hours.

More Realistic Training #

Artificial intelligence could also improve the quality of hands-on training as well. Realistic scenarios that even come close to the immense complexity of the real worldareexceedingly difficult to build, run, and maintain. So much so, in fact, that they do not exist. Michael Schwille, Scott Fisher and Eli Albright recently discussed this in We Wanted to Implement Data-Driven Operations During an Army Exercise — Here’s What We Learned. As Rudy and Arnaud pointed out in 2019, though, “If the corresponding data exists and canbeacquired, a cyberteammate has the capacity to simulate any type of environment, whether friendly, neutral, or adversarial.” An artificial intelligence agent could manage this entire function, from generating the underlying code and then deploying it in an Infrastructure as Code capacity to creating and managing complex scenarios with synthetic adversary activity that responds to the actions of the training audience in real time. No longer must analysts suffer through small, contrived exercises based on canned scripts put on by under resourced training cells.

Therearevaluable roles for artificial intelligence to play in cyber operations. In How Will AI Change Cyber Operations?, Jenny Jun captured this well when she said “Sharper Swords, Tougher Shields.” Much of that value today, however, lies in defensive cyber operations. As a cyber analyst, I have access to hundreds of billions of new records every day — a prime target for machine learning. An analyst support tool, built on top of a large language model, could further accelerateConsider replacing with: speed up my pace of analysis. In the lead up to those operations, artificial intelligence could help lessen the crushing burden of training development and execution.
