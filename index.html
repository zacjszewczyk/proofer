Artificial Intelligence at the Tactical Cyber Edge

Back in 2019, Rudy Guyonneau and Arnaud Le Dez captured a common fear in Artificial Intelligence in Digital Warfare: “The question of AI now tends to manifest under the guise of a mythicized omniscience and therefore, of a mythicized omnipotence. This can lead to paralysis of people fearful of having to fight against some super-enemy endowed with such an intelligence that it would leave us bereft of solutions.” In 2022, when ChatGPT exploded onto the scene, it looked like that fear had come true. Rather than falling hard from the “Peak of Inflated Expectations” and getting stuck in the “Trough of Disillusionment”, though, these case studies should drive us up the “Slope of Enlightenment.” Therearevaluable roles for artificial intelligence to play in cyber operations today. Much of that value, however, lies in defensive capability. Through more rapid analysis, improved warning intelligence, more efficient education, and more realistic training, artificial intelligence could have a drastic impact on defensive cyber operations today.

The State of Play of Artificial Intelligence in Cyber Operations #

Microsoft and OpenAI found five threat actors from four countries using large language models, one of the most common forms of artificial intelligence today. At the start of their campaigns, the groups used them for target research, as translators, to build tools, and to write content for phishing campaigns. Later, Microsoft saw the models guiding post-compromise actions. Although some research has proposed that modern models could take on more, most disagree. Rather than the letdown of an over-hyped promise, though, we ought to view these case studies as early examples of proper uses for artificial intelligence in offensive cyber operations. In stark contrast to ideas that artificial intelligence would unleash a wave of robot hackers on the world, these actors used the tools to “automate the boring stuff”, as Al Sweigart says. Defensive cyber forces, on the other hand, have an unrealized opportunity to use this technology to great effect. While use cases abound, artificial intelligence could have the most impact by accelerating the pace of analysis.

Improved Warning Intelligence #

Machine learning applied to publicly available internet-wide scanning data presents another interesting opportunity to derive meaning from a massive, overwhelming sea of data. Traditional intelligence processes have struggled to keep pace with the cyber domain. As then-Defense Intelligence Agency Director LTG Scott Berrier explained at the 13th Annual Billington Cybersecurity Summit in 2022, “If you think about foundational military intelligence, it’s based on understanding what the foreign militaries have, what their capabilitiesare based on the physical presence of these things. It’s harder in cyber because you may know where a cyber facility physicallyislocated, but you really don’t know what activityisgoing on inside that facility.” By identifying past servers used to launch attacks, command and control malware implants, or receive exfiltrated data, though, and training models to recognize those configuration patterns, defensive cyber analysts could then deploy those models against live data feeds to proactively identify new malicious infrastructure in near-real time. Rather than acting on similar insights in days or weeks as reports make their way out of an intelligence cycle, this strategy presents a promising avenue to operationalize intelligence in the cyber domain at machine speed.
